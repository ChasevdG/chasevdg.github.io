The Spherical Swarm

Three ways a crowd of agents can self-organize on the surface of a sphere. Pick a model, then tune the coupling and watch order emerge. All three live on the same 2-sphere but differ in what each agent carries and what it agrees on.

What's the difference?

Lohe oscillators generalize the Kuramoto model to the sphere: each agent is a unit vector that spins about the vertical axis at its own natural frequency $\omega_i$, while coupling $K$ pulls every vector toward the mean field. With uniform $\omega_i=1$ (a shared axis and rate) the whole swarm co-rotates and locks into a single precessing cluster at any $K$; with normal-distributed frequencies each vector precesses about its own random axis, so the coupling must fight the spread — exactly the Kuramoto competition — and only large $K$ pulls them into one cluster. Order parameter $r=\lvert\frac1N\sum_i \mathbf{x}_i\rvert$ runs from 0 (scattered) to 1 (one cluster).

Swarmalators add an internal phase $\theta_i$ (shown as colour) that co-evolves with position. Space coupling makes agents aggregate; the term $J\cos(\theta_j-\theta_i)$ makes that attraction depend on phase similarity, while phase coupling $K$ syncs colours of nearby agents. Positive $J$ gives a synced ball of one colour; negative $J$ splits space by phase into coloured patches — behaviour Lohe sync cannot produce.

Flocking (Vicsek) agents instead carry a velocity and move along geodesics, steering each step toward the average heading of their neighbours (plus noise). They agree on a direction of motion, not a location, so order shows up as migrating flocks and rotating polar vortices. The order parameter here is the polarization $\lvert\frac1N\sum_i \mathbf{v}_i\rvert$.
