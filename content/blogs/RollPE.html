In this blog post, I will go over my workshop paper on positional encoding using traveling waves or more concretely, as a Roll operations. One insight is that RollPE explicitly ties dimensions of the query space together and imposes a topology. This performs significantly better than the naive learned positional encoding and about as well as RoPE. The Roll operation can, in fact, be rewritten as a form of RoPE. While the method itself is limited, but offers an interesting perspective as a stepping stone into understanding RoPE as a Hamiltonian flow.

Biological Motivation

Traveling waves have been reported since the early days of neurophysiology, but their precise functional role remains highly debated. This ongoing debate is largely a biproduct of the demanding precision needed to measure them. Characterizing traveling waves requires both high spatial and high temporal resolution simultaneously. Most methods trade one off against the other: electrophysiology resolves millisecond timing but samples sparsely in space, while imaging methods cover space densely but slowly. Because of these limitations, the whether waves play an active role in the brain's computations or are simply a biproduct of the individual spikes remains debated. While I will take the stance that brain waves -- whether induced by synaptic spikes or a field of its own -- are the brains way of sequence ordering, i.e. positional encoding in the language of transformers.

Medial Septum

To support my hypothesis, it is useful to look at the hippocampus, the region responsible for spatial learning, navigation and memory formation. Its theta rhythm is coordinated by the medial septum, a structure in the basal forebrain that projects densely to the hippocampus and is widely believed to pace activity across it. The medial septum is composed of so-called "pacemaker" neurons which, like the heart, pulse at a frequency without any external stimuli -- in this case 4-12Hz . This frequency aligns with the famous theta oscillation waves in the hippocampus.

When the region is lesioned or inactivated, these theta oscillations are eliminated or severely disrupted, and spatial memory fails . This suggests that the medial septum acts as an important mechanism for coordinating the "clocks" across the hippocampus. Crucially, it was found that spatial learning and memory were impaired and the periodicity of grid cells in the entorhinal cortex degraded when theta was reduced . Degeneration of the medial septum's cholinergic neurons is in fact an early feature of Alzheimer's disease, and is linked to disrupted theta and impaired memory restoring the theta frequency can even rescue memory deficits in disease models.

Place Cells and Grid Cells

Place Cells

Edvard and May-Britt Moser -- in conjunction with John O'Keefe -- won the nobel prize for the discovery of two types of "GPS" cells: place cells and grid cells. O'Keefe discovered that place cells when an animal was at specific locations in an environment. Importantly, place cells can be found in the hippocampus and are highly plastic and remap to the animal's environment. While place cells have concrete connections to locations in an environment, there is also theories and evidence that they encode positions of other animals and positioning in more abstract spaces.

Grid Cells

By contrast, rather than firing at particular locations, grid cells fire periodically across locations often forming a hexagonal grid. Though the grid cells coherence was affected by the removal of the medial septum, they are not located in the hippocampus. Rather grid cells can be found in the entirhonal cortex -- which feeds into to the hippocampus, but is functionally distinct. It is fairly accepted that grid cells contribute to place cells, but the reciprical interaction of place cells on grid cells is less certain. Though in this blog post we take the perspective that stimuli combine with grid cells to give rise to place cells, the reader should take biological validity of that perspective with a grain of salt.

In addition to place cells and grid cells animals have also been shown to have head direction cells -- which live on a ring/circle -- and border detecting cells. Bats have been shown to have 3-dimensional place cells and have 3-dimensional head direction cells corresponding to the three Euler angles -- yaw, roll and pitch. Interestingly, they found yaw (azimuthal) and pitch head direction cells on a bat correspond to two independent rings -- i.e. a 2D torus -- rather than a sphere. Furthermore, their lab showed that, while hexagonal grid cells formed for bats on the ground, while flying grid cells -- or what seemingly corresponded to grid cells -- had less clear structure . They also found that, in bats, the grid cells did not need theta oscillations .

For this blog post, we will stick to 1D and 2D data because it is better understood both in terms of neuroscience and tesselation theory. We will choose to ignore all the contrary evidence that point to oscillatory behavior being epiphenomenal 😜

Neuro-inspired Positionally Encoding

Because the brain seems to rely on oscillations and grid cells for positionally encoding, it is interesting to see if positional encoding in artificial networks can benefit from these mechanisms. First, we will go over using traveling waves to encode position. We will start by using the cyclic permutation, or Roll, operator to perform discrete traveling waves before generalizing to a continuous roll operation. Afterwards, we will create positional encodings for 2D images which reproduce the hexagonal grid structure of biological grid cells.

Positionally Encoding with Traveling Waves

We will define a traveling wave as a function, or waveform, $u(x,t) = \phi(x + \omega t)$, where $\omega$ is the speed at which the wave is traveling and $t$ is the position. In contrast, a stationary or standing wave would be defined by $u(x,t) = \phi(x)\cos(\omega t + \delta)$, though any standing wave can be created by two traveling waves traveling in opposite directions. Prototypical examples are shown on the right.

Our main goal will to be to treat queries and keys as traveling waves. In the context of the brain, this would be tagging each memory "key" with a time stamp by letting the signal travel a certain distance based on time. Every "query to memory" will check for whether its query-value (also propagated forward) matches the key.

RollPE

Because we are dealing with transformers which have query and key values which are vector valued instead of continuous functions, we will rely on the Roll operator. Colloquially, the Roll operator "rolls" the coefficients in the vector down one position.

A Roll can be defined using the cyclic permutation matrix, $$ \text{Roll}(\vec{q},1) = \begin{pmatrix} 0 & 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 \\ 0 & 0 & 1 & \cdots & 0 \\ \vdots & \vdots & \ddots & \ddots & \vdots \\ 0 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 1 \\ 1 & 0 & \cdots & 0 & 0 \end{pmatrix} \vec{q} $$ We will define the cyclic permutation matrix as $\mathbf{S}$. Rolling $t$ times ammounts to $\mathbf{S}$ $t$ times, whcih allows us to define rolling a vector for $t$ time steps as $$ \text{Roll}(\vec{q},t) = \mathbf{S}^t \vec{q} $$ This allows us to define a positional encoding by applying the Roll operations based on the position of the word, as shown in Fig ... .

Relative Positional Encoding Property

As long as the boundary conditions of the traveling wave are cyclic, RollPE is a relative positional encoding -- i.e. the attention score between two tokens depends on the distance between them and not their global positions. To prove this mathematically, notice that the attention is given by the inner-product of the query. $$ \vec{q}^{T}\vec{k} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} q_n k_n $$ we can define $\bar{\mathbf{q}}$ and $\bar{\mathbf{k}}$ as the original query and key before the Roll. $$ \vec{\bar{q}}^{T}\vec{\bar{k}} = \sum_{n=0}^{N-1} \bar{q}_{n+\omega p_q} \bar{k}_{n+\omega p_k} $$ Finally, we can rewrite the indices of the sum, $$ \vec{\bar{q}}^{T}\vec{\bar{k}} = \sum_{n=p_k}^{N + p_k -1} \bar{q}_{i+\omega p_q - \omega p_k} \bar{k}_{i} $$ Since the domain is cyclic, we sum over the full domain, so $$ \vec{\bar{q}}^{T}\vec{\bar{k}} = \sum_{n=p_k}^{N + p_k -1} \bar{q}_{i+\omega (p_q - p_k)} \bar{k}_{n} = \sum_{n=0}^{N - 1} \bar{q}_{n+\omega (p_q - p_k)} \bar{k}_{n} $$ so the attention only depends on $p_q-p_k$.

The matrix form offers a proof which is more simple and clean $$ \vec{q}^{\top}\vec{k} = \bar{\mathbf{q}}^{\top} {\mathbf{S}^{p_q}}^\top {\mathbf{S}^{p_k}} \bar{\mathbf{k}} $$ Because $\mathbf{S}$ is an orthonormal matrix, ${\mathbf{S}^p}^{\top} = \mathbf{S}^{-p}$, so the equation simplifies to $$ \vec{q}^{\top}\vec{k} = \bar{\mathbf{q}}^{\top} {\mathbf{S}^{-(p_q - p_k)}} \bar{\mathbf{k}}, $$ once again being dependent only on the relative position $p_q - p_k$.

Continuous RollPE

One of the problems with RollPE is that the traveling wave has a periodicity proportional to the length of the query vector. While this can be sufficient for data with a small set of positions, this is rather limiting for language, where context length can reach the millions. We can generalize to continuous positions by using differential operators.

If we take our query to be analytic (Taylor expansion converges), then we can expand the traveling wave via its Taylor expansion, $$ q(x + v t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{(v t)^n}{n!} \frac{d^n}{d x^n} q(x), $$ which we can rewrite as, $$ q(x + v t) = \sum_{n=0}^{\infty} \frac{1}{n!} \left( v t \frac{d}{d x} \right)^n q(x), $$ which can be rewritten as, $$ q(x + v t) = e^{v t \frac{d}{d x}} q(x). $$

Using the Fourier identity, $\frac{d}{dx} = \mathcal{F}^{-1} (i \omega) \mathcal{F}$, where $\omega$ is the Fourier frequency, and using $e^{\mathcal{F}^{-1} \Lambda \mathcal{F}} = \mathcal{F}^{-1} e^{\Lambda} \mathcal{F}$, $$ q(x + v t) = \mathcal{F}^{-1} e^{i v t \omega} \mathcal{F} q(x). $$ This lets us define a continuous translation by Fourier transforming $q$, multiplying appropriately, and inverting the Fourier transform. (In fact, this identity holds under far weaker assumptions than analyticity: the shift theorem gives it directly whenever the Fourier transform exists.)

It is expensive to Fourier transform forward and back, but one could instead treat the query as already Fourier transformed. In addition, by Plancherel's theorem (taking the unitary convention for $\mathcal{F}$), the inner product computed in Fourier space is the same as in the original space. Working on a periodic domain with discrete modes $q_n$, $k_n$ of frequency $\omega_n$, this allows us to compute the attention score as $$ \mathrm{Att}(q,k) = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \overline{\left(e^{i \omega_n p_q} q_n\right)}^{\top} e^{i \omega_n p_k} k_n = \sum_{n=-\infty}^{\infty} \bar{q}_n^{\top} e^{i \omega_n (p_k - p_q)} k_n, $$ where $\omega_n = \frac{n}{2\pi N}$. This form makes it clear that it is relative, since it once again depends only on the relative position $p_k - p_q$.

If we assume that the original query/key function is real valued, we can assume assume $q_n = q_{-n}$ and $k_n = k_{-n}$ allowing us to restrict ourselves to $n>0$ and taking only the real part of the resulting inner product. Since we again are limited to fixed length vectors of size $N$ in the transformer, we get the final attention score given by $$ \mathrm{Att}(q,k) = \mathrm{Re} \left[ \sum_{n=0}^{N} \bar{q}_n^{\top} e^{i \omega_n (p_k - p_q)} k_n \right]. $$ While one could keep the first $N$ Fourier frequencies, $\omega_n = \frac{n}{2\pi N}$, which $N$ frequencies to choose can be left to design choice. The astute reader will recognize this equation as a precisely the form of attention given by RoPE.

Rather than using the Fourier basis and RoPE, we could instead treat the query as a function directly. We typically obtain the query by applying an linear transformation to the token content $\vec{q}_i = W_q \vec{x}_i$. We could instead treat the query as a vector which represents a continuous function sampled at $N$ sensor points. We can then create our query using a conditional coordinate MLP, $$ \vec{q}_{i, n} = \text{MLP}(\vec{x}_i, n) $$ where $n$ is the sensor point, i.e. index of the vector. We can then create a continuous traveling wave by transforming the sensor locations, $$ \vec{q}_{i, n} = \text{MLP}(\vec{x}_i, n + \omega p_i). $$ For an (approximately) relative positional encoding, we our sensor points need to span the entire domain of the query function, so we can apply a modulo operation, $$ \vec{q}_{i, n} = \text{MLP}(\vec{x}_i, (n + \omega p_i) \bmod N). $$ This just replaces the standard linear transformation to get query and keys, but the attention remains the same. It is probably not expensive since it will be linear in number of tokens -- compared with the quadratic cost of attention -- and it should be easy to implement. SiRENs also impose smoothness on the query function, which is probably a good inductive bias due to the gaps in sensor locations. Because of these gaps, the attention is not perfectly relative, but it should be close with smoothness.

Grid Cell Positional Encodings

Independent waves can serve as positional encodings, but doing so is equivalent to Axial Rotary positional encodings, which prior work has shown to be sub-optimal. Instead of encoding 2D positions with traveling waves, we turn to a different source of inspiration: grid cells.

Grid cells fire in a hexagonal lattice that tiles the environment, giving the animal a metric for path integration -- the ability to track its own position through space without external cues. A square lattice could tile the same space, but the hexagonal arrangement is mathematically optimal, achieving the highest spatial resolution for the least redundancy. This may explain why the hexagonal lattice emerges consistently when agents are trained to path integrate in 2D .

Crucially for our purposes, a hexagonal lattice can be constructed by superposing three grating patterns oriented 120 degrees apart -- a form directly amenable to use as a positional encoding.

We can construct our hexagonal positional encoding by first starting with RoPE for 2D from . We can define Mixed RoPE as $$ \mathrm{Att}(q,k) = \mathrm{Re}\left[\sum_{n=0}^{N/2} \bar{q}_n^{\top} e^{i \vec{\omega}_n^{\top} (\vec{p}_k - \vec{p}_q)} k_n\right] $$ where $\mathbf{\omega}_n$ represents the "wave vector" whose direction controls the orientation of the grating pattern, and whose magnitude controls the frequency of the grating pattern.

To create a hexagonal lattice, we can superpose three grating patterns oriented 120 degrees apart. We can define the wave vectors as $$ \vec{\omega}_{n,m} = \begin{pmatrix} \cos(\theta_{m}) \\ \sin(\theta_{m}) \end{pmatrix} \omega_n $$ where $\theta_{m} = m\frac{2\pi}{3}$ for $m=0,1,2$ and $\omega_n$ is the frequency of the $n$-th grating pattern.

Our overall attention score is then given by $$ \mathrm{Att}(q,k) = \mathrm{Re}\left[\sum_{m=0}^{2} \sum_{n=0}^{N/6} \bar{q}_{n,m}^{\top} e^{i \vec{\omega}_{n,m}^{\top} (\vec{p}_k - \vec{p}_q)} k_{n,m}\right]. $$ To create the hexagonal lattice, we need equal amplitude across orientations: $q_{n,0} = q_{n,1} = q_{n,2}$ and $k_{n,0} = k_{n,1} = k_{n,2}$ for all $n$. However, I found that to be too strict and empirically found it better to leave the model to learn the amplitudes of each orientation independently. This also makes it easier to implement as it just becomes Mixed RoPE with weight sharing across the three orientations.

Related Work

Platonic Transformer

The Platonic Transformer has a similar attention construction. Rather than rotating the wave vector, our method can equivalently be viewed as rotating the position vector, since rotating the position is equivalent to rotating the wave vector in the opposite direction. This amounts to "projecting" the position vector into three frames of reference, each rotated $120^\circ$ apart. More generally, one could project onto frames defined by any regular polygon -- a square ($90^\circ$ apart), a pentagon ($72^\circ$ apart) -- or, in more than two dimensions, onto frames defined by a Platonic solid: a tetrahedron, cube, octahedron, dodecahedron, or icosahedron.

This is essentially what the Platonic Transformer does: for each frame $R$ in a discrete subgroup $G \subset O(n)$, it projects positions as $p_i(R) = R^{-1}p_i$ and runs ordinary RoPE-attention within that frame, treating the $|G|$ frames as $|G|$ parallel attention heads. The motivations differ though.

Their frame set $G$ is chosen for full roto-reflection equivariance, not spatial coverage -- $G$ is a Platonic solid rotation group (12, 24, or 60 elements for the tetrahedron, octahedron, icosahedron) or a cyclic/dihedral group in 2D, and achieving that equivariance further requires lifting scalar and vector features to functions on $G$ and constraining every linear layer in the network to be a $G$-equivariant group convolution (their Sections 3.1--3.3). Since we are not motivated by equivariance, we can use a much smaller frame set -- just three frames in 2D -- and do not need to constrain the network architecture at all.

nD-RoPE

Even more closely related is nD-RoPE , which reaches nearly the same construction from a parallel derivation. Starting from the same translation-invariant, Fourier-based argument, they show a good wave-vector set $\Omega$ must satisfy coverage ($\text{rank}(\Omega) = n$, so no displacement direction is invisible to the phase system) and maximum symmetry (the induced real-space pattern shouldn't privilege coordinate axes). They prove that under minimal redundancy, the unique configuration meeting both is the centered regular $(n+1)$-vertex simplex -- for $n=2$, exactly three wave vectors $120^\circ$ apart, reproducing the same hexagonal lattice we get (their Fig. 3 shows this explicitly).
