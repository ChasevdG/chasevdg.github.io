The Landau–Stuart oscillator. The simplest system that spontaneously starts to oscillate: a single complex ODE that is the normal form of the supercritical Hopf bifurcation. Tune one parameter through zero and a stable fixed point gives way to a stable limit cycle. This post embeds a live demo showing the vector field and short trajectory streaks as the parameters change.

The Model

The Landau–Stuart (or Stuart–Landau) model describes a complex amplitude $z(t) \in \mathbb{C}$ evolving as $$\dot{z} = (\mu + i\omega)\,z - |z|^2 z,$$ where $\mu$ controls the linear growth rate and $\omega$ the rotation frequency. In polar coordinates $z = r e^{i\theta}$ the dynamics decouple: $$\dot{r} = \mu r - r^3, \qquad \dot{\theta} = \omega.$$ The radial equation makes the phase portrait easy to read. For $\mu \le 0$ the only attractor is the origin: every trajectory spirals into the fixed point $r = 0$. For $\mu > 0$ the origin becomes unstable and a stable limit cycle appears at $r = \sqrt{\mu}$ — trajectories inside the circle spiral outward onto it, trajectories outside spiral inward. The transition at $\mu = 0$ is the supercritical Hopf bifurcation, and because every generic Hopf bifurcation reduces to this equation near onset, the Landau–Stuart model shows up everywhere from fluid instabilities to coupled-oscillator networks.

Live Demo

The demo shows the vector field (orange arrows) together with brief trajectory streaks integrated on the fly: crimson while a trajectory is inside the attracting circle, green outside. The attractor itself is drawn in solid orange — a ring at $r = \sqrt{\mu}$ when $\mu > 0$, collapsing to a point at the origin when $\mu \le 0$. Drag $\mu$ below zero to watch the limit cycle vanish through the Hopf bifurcation, set $\omega$ negative to reverse the rotation, or set $\omega = 0$ to see purely radial flow onto the attractor.

Coupled Oscillators

Because the Landau–Stuart equation is the normal form of any oscillator near onset, it is the natural building block for studying collections of interacting oscillators. Coupling $N$ of them diffusively through their mean field, $$\dot{z}_j = (\mu + i\omega_j)\,z_j - |z_j|^2 z_j + K\,(\langle z \rangle - z_j), \qquad \langle z \rangle = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{k=1}^{N} z_k,$$ with natural frequencies $\omega_j$ spread over a width $\Delta\omega$, produces two competing effects. Coupling pulls the oscillators toward their common mean field, so for $K$ large relative to $\Delta\omega$ the ensemble phase-locks and rotates as one — the amplitude analogue of Kuramoto synchronization. But unlike phase-only models, the amplitudes can also react: the diffusive term $-Kz_j$ acts as extra damping, and when both $K$ and $\Delta\omega$ are large the oscillators pull each other off their limit cycles entirely and collapse into the origin. This amplitude death is a genuinely amplitude-driven effect — the origin, unstable for every isolated oscillator, is stabilized by the coupling (for two oscillators, when $K > 1$ and $\Delta\omega > 2\sqrt{2K - 1}$).
