Synchronizing applause with the Kuramoto model. When an audience claps, individual clappers with different natural tempos can spontaneously fall into a common rhythm. This is a textbook instance of the Kuramoto model of coupled phase oscillators. This post embeds a live demo: each pair of hands is an oscillator, a monitor spikes on every clap, and the phases are shown on the complex unit circle. It also demonstrates that subtab-about-image blocks can embed a live demo straight from its own standalone HTML file.

The Model

Each clapper $i$ carries a phase $\theta_i(t)$ that advances at its natural frequency $\omega_i$, and the displayed animation frame is a pure function of that phase: the hands close as $\bigl(\tfrac{1-\cos\theta_i}{2}\bigr)^{0.6}$ and a clap fires each time $\theta_i$ crosses $\pi \pmod{2\pi}$. Coupling ties the ensemble together through the mean field $$r e^{i\psi} = \frac{1}{N}\sum_{j=1}^{N} e^{i\theta_j},$$ giving the dynamics $$\dot{\theta}_i = \omega_i + K r \sin(\psi - \theta_i).$$ The order parameter $r \in [0,1]$ measures coherence: $r \approx 0$ for scattered phases, $r = 1$ for perfect sync. For two oscillators, phase-locking requires $K > |\omega_1 - \omega_2| / 2$; below threshold the phases drift past each other forever. Because the coupling strength each oscillator feels is proportional to $r$, synchronization is self-reinforcing — once a few clappers lock, $r$ grows and drags in the stragglers.

Kuramoto on Networks

The mean-field model assumes every oscillator talks to every other. On a network, each oscillator only couples to its neighbors, $$\dot{\theta}_i = \omega_i + \frac{K}{\deg(i)} \sum_{j \in \mathcal{N}(i)} \sin(\theta_j - \theta_i),$$ and the topology decides how synchronization spreads: on a 2D grid or 1D line, locking grows in local patches whose domain walls slowly anneal; on an Erdős–Rényi graph, random shortcuts let coherence spread globally at much lower $K$; hyperbolic graphs sit in between, with hubs that entrain their periphery. Kuramoto on the Sphere

The clapping model lives on the circle — each phase $\theta_i$ is a point on $S^1$. The Lohe model lifts the same idea to the 2-sphere: each oscillator becomes a unit vector $\mathbf{x}_i \in S^2$ that precesses at its own natural frequency $W_i$ and is pulled toward the mean field, $$\dot{\mathbf{x}}_i = W_i \mathbf{x}_i + K\,\mathrm{P}_{\mathbf{x}_i}\!\Big(\tfrac1N\sum_j \mathbf{x}_j\Big),$$ where $\mathrm{P}_{\mathbf{x}_i}$ projects onto the tangent plane so the vector stays on the sphere. With uniform frequencies the swarm co-rotates and locks trivially; with normal-distributed frequencies the spread competes with $K$, reproducing the Kuramoto synchronization transition one dimension up. The demo also contrasts this pure synchronization with two richer relatives — swarmalators, which couple an internal phase to spatial position, and Vicsek flocking, where agents align velocities rather than positions.

Huygens' Pendulums

The oldest recorded observation of spontaneous synchronization is Christiaan Huygens' 1665 account of two pendulum clocks hanging from a common beam falling into step — the "odd kind of sympathy" that Kuramoto's model would formalize three centuries later. Here $N$ pendulums hang from a shared beam; each carries a phase $\theta_i$ obeying the mean-field Kuramoto dynamics $$\dot{\theta}_i = \omega_i + K r \sin(\psi - \theta_i),$$ and swings as $\varphi_i = A \sin\theta_i$. The beam plays the role of the coupling medium: it sways with the mean field, so it barely moves while the phases are scattered and rocks visibly once the ensemble locks.

Aside: The Forward-Forward Algorithm

A different kind of local, self-organizing dynamics: Hinton's Forward-Forward algorithm replaces backprop's forward + backward pass with two forward passes. An MNIST digit is fed through the network twice — once with the correct label written into its first pixels (positive data), once with a wrong label (negative data). Each layer optimizes only its own objective $p = \sigma\bigl(\sum_j y_j^2 - \theta\bigr)$, raising its goodness (the sum of squared activations) above the threshold $\theta$ on positive data and pushing it below on negative data. No gradients travel between layers; each learns locally.
